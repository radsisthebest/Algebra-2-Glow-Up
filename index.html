How to use this sheet

Find the topic, read “Use it when…,” then copy the example with your question’s numbers. Opening this sheet never lowers your score.

Quadratics

Use it when: you see x² and need the solutions.

x = (−b ± √(b²−4ac)) ÷ 2a

a, b, c are the numbers in ax²+bx+c=0.

Example: x²−5x+6=0 factors to (x−2)(x−3), so x=2 or 3.

Discriminant: b²−4ac. Positive = 2 real answers; zero = 1; negative = no real answers.

Exponential functions

Use it when: the variable is in the exponent.

y = a(b)ˣ

a = starting value. b = growth factor. b>1 means growth; 0<b<1 means decay.

Example: 3·2ˣ=48 → 2ˣ=16 → x=4.

Tip: isolate the power first, then rewrite both sides with the same base.

Logarithms

Use it when: you need to find an exponent.

logₐ(b)=x means aˣ=b

Example: log₂(32)=5 because 2⁵=32.

Product: log(xy)=log x+log y

Quotient: log(x/y)=log x−log y

Power: log(xᵖ)=p·log x

Warning: you can only take a log of a positive number.

Polynomials

Use it when: dividing, finding factors, or reading how a graph meets the x-axis.

Remainder Theorem: dividing p(x) by x−a leaves remainder p(a).

Example: p(x)=x²+1 divided by x−3 has remainder p(3)=10.

Factor test: x−a is a factor when p(a)=0.

Odd multiplicity crosses the x-axis; even multiplicity touches and turns.

Rational functions

Use it when: x appears in a fraction’s denominator.

First: factor and simplify.

Hole: comes from a factor that canceled.

Vertical asymptote: set the remaining denominator equal to zero.

Example: (x−2)/[(x−2)(x+1)] has a hole at x=2 and an asymptote at x=−1.

Warning: canceled values are still excluded from the domain.

Radicals

Use it when: simplifying square roots or rational exponents.

x¹⁄ⁿ = ⁿ√x

Example: √72=√(36·2)=6√2.

Tip: look for the largest perfect-square factor.

Warning: after solving a radical equation, substitute answers back to check for extras.

Sequences

Arithmetic adds the same amount; geometric multiplies by the same amount.

Arithmetic: aₙ=a₁+(n−1)d

Example: 5,8,11,… has a₈=5+7(3)=26.

Geometric: aₙ=a₁rⁿ⁻¹

Finite geometric sum: Sₙ=a₁(1−rⁿ)/(1−r)

Complex numbers & trig

Powers of i repeat: i, −1, −i, 1.

Example: i¹⁰=i²=−1 because 10 has remainder 2 after division by 4.

Degrees to radians: multiply by π/180.

Example: 60°·π/180=π/3.

sin²θ+cos²θ=1 and tanθ=sinθ/cosθ.
